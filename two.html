1. reasons for 2 to not exist

1.1. uni what the fuck are you talking about

In mathematics, there’s a bunch of ways infinities can be large. Usually, the existence of these can not be derived from the usual axioms yielding an important logical component to the study of these large cardinals. But many of these properties include the assumption that the cardinal number in question is infinite - once we remove that assumption (or sometimes restate the property entirely) we can see that 2 is, in many regards, a pretty huge number.

1.2. set-theoretical stuff

1.2.1. regularity

A cardinal number \(\kappa\) is regular if it cannot be expressed as an indexed sum of numbers smaller than \(\kappa\) where the indexing number is also smaller than \(\kappa\), i.e. there’s no index \(\iota < \kappa\) such that a family of sets \(A_i\) for \(i \in \iota\) exists for which \(\sum_{i \in \iota} A_i > \kappa\) holds.

For example, the cardinal \(\aleph_0\), as in the size of the natural numbers, is regular, since every finitely indexed sum of finite numbers is finite. However, the only finite regular cardinals are 0, 1 and 2 - every other natural number \(n\) can be expressed as the sum \(1 + (n-1)\), both of which are smaller than n.

Since regularity is an important part of the definition of inaccessible cardinals, which are indeed quite large, I guess this kind of makes 2 way larger in relationship to 0 and 1 at least.

1.3. categories

1.3.1. 2 is cartesian closed
